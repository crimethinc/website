#26: Anarcha-Feminism, Part I: Introduction and Herstory

Summary

What is anarcha-feminism, and what contribution has it made to both feminism and anarchism? In this episode we kick off a series exploring anarcha-feminism in the past and present. After framing the issue and dealing with some thorny questions around definitions of feminism and gender, we take a whirlwind tour through the history—or herstory, if you like—of anarchist women from the barricades of the Paris Commune to the front lines of the Spanish Civil War. In addition to Louise Michel, Lucy Parsons, Emma Goldman, and a few other big names, we’ll share stories of Russian nihilists, Puerto Rican tobacco workers, Japanese journalists, Mexican guerrillas, and many other unsung heroines of late 19th and early 20th century anarchist struggles. The anthology Quiet Rumors: An Anarcha-Feminist Reader appears on the Chopping Block, and a member of the Revolutionary Anarcha-Feminist Group from Dublin, Ireland joins us for an interview. Clara and Alanis even take issue with a term from the Contradictionary, along with a packed calendar of upcoming events, news, and more. {August 6, 2014}

  • In our introduction to the discussion of anarcha-feminism, we cited Wikipedia’s definition of feminism, critiques of equality, discussions of intersectional politics from an anarchist perspective; bell hooks, from Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center (also the source of the Cellestine Ware quote); Carol Ehrlich’s “Socialism, Anarchism, and Feminism,” ; a 1980s Norwegian anarcha-feminist statement; Deirdre Hogan’s essay “Anarcha-Feminism: Thinking About Anarchism”; differentiation between sex and gender and between assigned sex and gender identity, as well as critiques of sex, not just gender, as socially constructed and the concept of “not-man” as a non-essentialist, non-identitarian way to talk about subjectivity under patriarchy. Plus a lot of other stuff.

Now, on to the folks we discussed in our history of early anarchist women:

  • Mary Wollstonecraft: you can read Vindication of the Rights of Woman online; also see this BBC History discussion of her life and thought and Emma Goldman’s eulogy to her.

  • Louise Michel: If you can find her autobiography The Red Virgin, it’s fantastic; there’s also a book called Louise Michel: Rebel Lives which you can read online. Also see her “Memories of the Commune”, and especially this transcript from an 1883 trial for leading a violent demonstration that looted a bakery—if you need any more confirmation of what an unrelenting badass she was.

  • Russian nihilist women: Vera Figner, Vera Zasulich, and Sophia Perovskaya. Kropotkin mentions his sister-in-law in his Memoirs of a Revolutionist—though doesn’t bother to give her name, that sexist bugger.

  • Lucy Parsons: a great place to start is the short article in Rolling Thunder #4; for an in-depth but flawed study, see Carolyn Ashbaugh’s biography Lucy Parsons: American Revolutionary, though first check out the helpful corrective “A Fury for Justice: Lucy Parsons and the Revolutionary Anarchist Movement in Chicago”. Some of her writings are available through The Anarchist Library and the Anarchy Archives. There’s also the Lucy Parsons Center in Boston, Massachusetts, which we profiled in our Mugshot in Episode 1.

  • Emma Goldman: Her memoir Living My Life, from which we quoted, remains one of the best anarchist books ever written, and an indispensible glimpse into the radical worlds of the late 19th and early 20th century. A ton of her writings are on The Anarchist Library, and there are several biographies of her, including Richard Drinnon’s Rebel in Paradise: A Biography of Emma Goldman, Candace Falk’s Love, Anarchy, and Emma Goldman, Alice Wexler’s Emma Goldman: An Intimate Life and sections of Paul Avrich’s fantastic Anarchist Portraits. PBS did an “American Experience” documentary on Goldman; their page of further reading suggestions has a ton of useful sources.

  • And then we began a panoramic view of many of the different ways women engaged with anarchist struggles. As labor activists: Sophie Vasilio, the Chilean Working Women’s Union Federation, Milly Witkop, the Bolivian Women’s Labor Federation, and Rose Pesotta. As creators of infrastructure for women’s resistance: Virginia Bolten and La Voz De La Mujer, Uruguayan “Sociedades de Resistencia” for working women, Voltairine de Cleyre’s “Philadelphia Ladies Liberal League,” He Zhen’s “Women’s Rights Recovery Association,” the Brazilian “Women’s Education Center” founded by Angelina Soares, the Portuguese “Louise Michel Libertarian Women’s Group,” and the Spanish “Mujeres Libres.”, and Teresa Torelles Espina. As educators, Malvina Tavares and Dorvalina Ribas, Lola Ridge, Louise Olivereau, Puerto Rican Social Studies Centers, and Elise Ottesen-Jensen. As authors and poets, Virgilia D’Andrea, Lola Ridge, Noe Ito, and Luisa Capetillo. As journalists and publishers, Florence Finch Kelly, Charlotte Wilson, Juana Belén, anarchist journals and newspapers across Latin America and the Caribbean, Kanno Sugako, Aniela Wolberg, Takamure Itsue, and Anna Edelstein Olay. As medical workers, Natasha Notkin, Madeline Pelletier, Dr. Marie Equi. Surreptitiously supporting militant strikers: Aurora Novoa Lozano. As guerillas, militiawomen, and terrorists: Olga Taratuta, Margarita Ortega, Clara Thallmann, and Maria Nikiforova.

  • Some of the other sources we drew on were Anarchist Women: 1870–1920 by Margaret Marsh (a feminist but non-anarchist account; helpful for source material, especially on American individualists, but otherwise highly problematic) and Anarchy! An Anthology of Emma Goldman’s Mother Earth. On Puerto Rico, Kirwin Shaffer’s Black Flag Boricuas; on Portugal, Freedom Fighters by Joao Freire; on Chinese anarchism and feminism, Peter Yarrow’s Anarchism and Chinese Political Culture. Online, the Anarcha Library, the Kate Sharpley Library, Kathy Ferguson’s website on “Emma Goldman’s Women”, and libcom.org’s history section all proved valuable.
